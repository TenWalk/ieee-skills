# Cover Letter (Editor-Facing)

The cover letter addresses the **editor**, not the reviewers. Its job is to let the editor decide
quickly that the paper fits the journal and is worth sending out for review. Keep it to one page.

## Structure (new submission)

```
[Date]

Dear Dr./Prof. [Editor name, or "Editor-in-Chief"],

[1] We submit our manuscript, "[Title]", for consideration as a [regular paper / letter /
    correspondence] in [Journal name].

[2] Problem & contribution: one short paragraph — the problem, why it matters, and what the paper
    contributes. State the result in one sentence with the key number if you have it.

[3] Fit: one or two sentences on why this belongs in *this* journal (scope/audience), optionally
    referencing the journal's topic areas.

[4] Originality & ethics statements: not previously published; not under review elsewhere; all
    authors approved; any conflicts / funding / data availability as the journal requires.

[5] Optional: suggested or opposed reviewers, if the journal invites them.

Thank you for your consideration.

Sincerely,
[Corresponding author, affiliation, email]
```

## Contribution framing (paragraph [2])

Mirror the Introduction's contribution list, compressed to prose. Lead with the gap, then the
move, then the payoff:

> Existing schemes for [problem] assume [limiting assumption], which fails when [condition]. We
> propose [scheme], which [key idea]. Under [channel/setting], it achieves [headline result],
> improving over the strongest benchmark scheme by [X dB / X bps/Hz] while [secondary advantage,
> e.g. lower complexity].

Do not paste the abstract. The editor will read the abstract; the cover letter is the human-to-human
pitch for why to act on it.

## Required statements (check the journal's exact wording)

Most IEEE journals expect some subset of:

- **Originality:** "This manuscript has not been published and is not under consideration
  elsewhere."
- **Authorship:** all authors have read and approved the submission.
- **Conflicts of interest:** declared, or "none".
- **Funding / acknowledgment of support**, if applicable.
- **Data / code availability**, if the journal has a policy.
- **Prior related work by the authors** (e.g., a conference version being extended) — disclose it
  and state what is new, since editors check for self-overlap.

If extending a prior conference paper, say so explicitly and quantify the new content (e.g., "this
extends our [venue] paper with a new algorithm, a closed-form performance analysis, and additional
simulations in Section V"). Undisclosed overlap is an integrity issue.

## Revised-submission cover letter

For a resubmission (major/minor revision), the cover letter is short and points to the response
letter rather than repeating it:

> We thank the editor and reviewers for their comments. We have revised the manuscript and submit
> it together with a point-by-point response letter detailing all changes. The principal revisions
> are: (1) ..., (2) ..., (3) .... We believe the manuscript is substantially strengthened.

Do not re-argue individual comments here — that is the response letter's job. Keep the headline
changes to three or four bullets of plain prose.

## Tone and length

- One page, professional, specific. No hype ("groundbreaking", "revolutionary").
- Name the journal and, where possible, the editor — a generic letter signals a scattershot
  submission.
- The cover letter makes claims the paper must support; do not promise results the manuscript does
  not contain.

## Bilingual author note

Draft freely in Chinese, then render the final letter in the paper's US-English register
(ieee-polishing). The editor reads only the English version.
